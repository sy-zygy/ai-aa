## Part 1: Plain-English Core Argument (2 Paragraphs)

*Use these paragraphs verbatim or adapt for social launch materials. They are legally reviewed and calibrated.*

> **The problem:** Millions of people with disabilities that affect reading and understanding — including dyslexia, ADHD, intellectual disabilities, traumatic brain injuries, and autism — depend on AI tools to comprehend documents that control their lives: medical discharge instructions, benefits letters, school enrollment forms, lease agreements. These tools summarize, explain, and translate complex text into plain language. But hospitals, schools, government agencies, and employers are increasingly blocking AI tools — through bot-detection systems, blanket bans, and copy-paste restrictions — without realizing they are cutting off an accessibility lifeline.
>
> **Why existing law already covers this:** The Americans with Disabilities Act requires organizations to provide "auxiliary aids and services" so people with disabilities can understand information that affects them. This category was designed to evolve with technology — it once meant TTY devices, then screen readers, then video captioning. AI tools that help someone understand their own medical records serve the same function for people with comprehension barriers as screen readers serve for blind users. No new law is needed. We are asking federal agencies to recognize what the statute already says: when someone with a disability uses AI to understand text they are already authorized to read, that is an auxiliary aid — and blocking it may constitute an accessibility barrier.

## Part 3: Key Legal Lines for Comms to Reuse

These are pre-approved phrasings that Communications can use freely across any content:

| Concept | Approved Phrasing |
|---------|-------------------|
| Core frame | "AI comprehension tools are the next auxiliary aid" |
| Legal basis | "The ADA already requires auxiliary aids for effective communication" |
| Technology evolution | "The law was designed to evolve with technology — screen readers were once novel too" |
| The barrier | "Blocking someone's AI comprehension tool may constitute an accessibility barrier" |
| Scope | "User-directed AI that helps someone understand text they already have the right to read" |
| What we're not | "We're not asking for new law — we're asking for existing law to be applied to modern tools" |
| Tagline | "Everyone deserves to understand" |

**Avoid these phrasings:**
- ~~"violates the ADA"~~ → use "may constitute a barrier" or "may violate"
- ~~"AI tools are auxiliary aids"~~ → use "AI tools **should be recognized as** auxiliary aids" or "**qualify as** auxiliary aids"
- ~~"institutions are breaking the law"~~ → use "institutions may be failing their effective-communication obligations"
- ~~"right to use AI"~~ → use "right to effective communication through auxiliary aids"
