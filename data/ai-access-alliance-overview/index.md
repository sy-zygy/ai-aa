# AI Access Alliance Overview

A sponsor-facing project charter built for a narrow first win and a much larger long-term vision. The core idea is to present the organization as the policy and civil-society infrastructure that will help AI become recognized as part of the accessibility stack for effective communication and comprehension. [^1] [^2]

## Charter

**Name:** AI Access Alliance (AI-AA)

**Slogan:** Ensuring AI-mediated comprehension is recognized as a legitimate accommodation under effective-communication law.

**Blue-sky vision:** establish the norm that people should not lose meaningful access to digital information, services, or participation because they rely on AI systems to understand, navigate, or mediate information.
**MVP mission:** win formal recognition that, in high-stakes text settings, blocking a person’s user-authorized AI comprehension tool can be an accessibility barrier under existing effective-communication and auxiliary-aids principles. [^1][^3][^2]

**Primary audience:** large AI companies, AI infrastructure firms, accessibility technology companies, and the public.
**Primary sponsor thesis:** this initiative will create the legal framing, technical norms, case studies, and public legitimacy that allow AI products to be adopted as trusted accessibility tools in healthcare, education, government, legal services, and employment.

## Program scope

The first-year project should stay tightly focused on “user-authorized AI comprehension aids” for high-stakes written information. That means the initial claim is about summarizing, explaining, defining, reformatting, and answering clarifying questions about text the user is already allowed to access, not about general bot rights or autonomous agent activity. [^1][^2][^4]


| Component | Year-one scope |
| :-- | :-- |
| Covered settings | Contracts, benefits notices, medical forms, school documents, employment paperwork, legal information. [^1][^3] |
| Covered need | Cognitive disability, neurodivergence, reading-related disability, or other communication/comprehension barriers. [^4] |
| Covered tools | User-directed AI that explains, summarizes, translates into plain language, and supports comprehension. [^4][^1] |
| Not included yet | Scraping rights, generalized interoperability mandates, autonomous action rights, shopping bots, trading agents, or universal access claims. |
| Policy ask | Agency guidance, model policies, pilots, and sector standards recognizing AI comprehension support as a possible effective-communication aid. [^1][^5][^2] |

The broader excluded issues should still appear in your public narrative as the long-term horizon. The group can say plainly that the first campaign is intentionally narrow so the law can move first on the most defensible and socially valuable use case. [^1][^3]

## Workstreams

The initiative should run through four coordinated workstreams. Each one needs a sponsor-visible output and a policy-facing output. [^1][^2]


| Workstream | Deliverables | Sponsor value |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Legal and policy | White paper, guidance petition drafts for DOJ/HHS/ED, model ADA/504 framing memo. [^1][^3][^2] | Creates a roadmap for lawful adoption in regulated sectors. |
| Evidence and research | User stories, expert advisory group, sector case studies, documented harm from blocked AI assistance, reliability safeguards. [^6] | Gives the issue legitimacy and answers safety objections. |
| Corporate standards | Model “allowable AI comprehension support” policy, technical design principles, trust-and-safety guardrails. | Helps sponsors convert advocacy into product and policy practice. |
| Coalition and public education | Disability-rights coalition, plain-language partners, legal-aid partners, explainer deck, launch site, media narrative. [^4] | Builds external validation and reduces the appearance of self-interested industry advocacy. |

A practical first-year set of milestones would be: publish the core doctrine memo, recruit an advisory council, secure 3 to 5 founding sponsors, launch one cross-sector coalition statement, and submit at least one formal guidance request to a civil-rights agency. [^1][^5][^2]
That is the real MVP because it converts an idea into an issue area with doctrine, stakeholders, institutional backers, and a visible ask. [^1]

## Funding and governance

Your funding model should be built around founding corporate sponsors, supported by public donations and smaller institutional backers. That pitch is credible because major AI companies already fund adjacent work: Microsoft announced a \$25 million AI for Accessibility program, Google.org launched a \$75 million AI Opportunity Fund in the United States, and OpenAI and Meta have nonprofit or accessibility-related support programs. [^7][^8]

A clean sponsor ladder would look like this:

- Founding sponsors: Microsoft, Google, OpenAI, Anthropic, Meta. [^7][^8][^9]
- Strategic supporters: accessibility companies, legal-tech firms, AI infrastructure providers, enterprise AI vendors.
- Public-interest layer: individual donors, disability-rights philanthropies, and law firms supporting access-to-justice or disability work.

For governance, you need a visible firewall between money and mission. The board or advisory council should include disabled and neurodivergent leaders, legal experts, and accessibility practitioners, and sponsor agreements should expressly deny funders control over legal positions, enforcement targets, or policy recommendations. [^6]
That governance design is important both ethically and strategically, because a corporate-funded accessibility group will otherwise be attacked as a front for platform interests rather than a rights-based initiative. [^6]

## Sponsor narrative

The sponsor deck should position the initiative as infrastructure for responsible market development. Microsoft’s accessibility program frames AI as a way to benefit people with disabilities, and Google.org’s AI Opportunity Fund emphasizes making AI opportunity broadly accessible through funding, training, and nonprofit support; your group extends that logic into civil-rights recognition and real-world institutional adoption.

A strong sponsor message would be:

- AI already helps people read, write, and communicate, and disability advocates are increasingly treating access to AI as a rights issue.
- High-stakes institutions still lack clear rules about when user-controlled AI support is a legitimate accessibility aid. [^1][^3][^2]
- That uncertainty slows adoption, raises compliance fears, and leaves users without support in the moments where comprehension matters most. [^1]
- This initiative will produce the doctrine, evidence, coalition backing, and implementation norms needed to make accessible AI support more lawful, trusted, and scalable. [^1][^2]

A concise external positioning line could be: “We help society recognize AI for what it already is becoming for many people: an accessibility layer for effective communication.” [^4][^1]
A concise sponsor line could be: “We create the policy and trust infrastructure that lets AI companies become durable partners in accessibility, not just tool vendors.” [^1]

## Startup Groundwork

AI-AA is a greenfield organization starting from zero. No funding, no volunteers, no web presence. The startup playbook treats social media impact as the engine that drives advocacy, public support, and corporate sponsorship simultaneously.

**The growth flywheel:** Human stories attract public attention. Public attention validates the cause. Validation attracts sponsors. Sponsors fund research and coalition-building. Research produces evidence. Evidence creates more stories.
