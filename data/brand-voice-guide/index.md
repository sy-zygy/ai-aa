# AI Access Alliance — Brand & Voice Guide

## Brand Identity

**Name:** AI Access Alliance (AI-AA)

**Tagline:** *Everyone deserves to understand.*

**Positioning statement:** AI Access Alliance is a disability-rights initiative that builds the legal, policy, and public infrastructure needed for AI comprehension tools to be recognized as legitimate accessibility accommodations. We are not a tech lobby. We are the bridge between civil-rights law and the AI tools people already depend on to understand the world around them.

**Brand personality:** Authoritative. Human. Urgent but measured. We lead with rights, not technology.

## Voice Principles

| Principle | What it means | Example |
|:--|:--|:--|
| Rights-first | Always frame AI access as a civil-rights issue, not a tech feature | "People deserve to understand their own medical records" not "AI can summarize documents" |
| Human-centered | Lead with people, not products | "Maria uses AI to read her lease" not "Our AI summarizes legal text" |
| Plain language | Practice what we preach — if we advocate for comprehension, our own content must be radically clear | No jargon walls. Grade 8 reading level for public content. |
| Credible, not combative | We build coalitions, not enemies. Even when challenging institutions, we frame it as "let's fix this together" | "Hospitals can lead on this" not "Hospitals are failing patients" |
| Specific, not vague | We name the settings, the laws, the people affected | "A veteran trying to understand their benefits letter" not "people with disabilities" |

## Audience Personas

### 1. Corporate Sponsor (Primary)

**Who:** CSR/Social Impact leads, Accessibility VPs, Policy teams at Microsoft, Google, OpenAI, Anthropic, Meta, and mid-tier AI companies.

**What they care about:**
- Alignment with their existing accessibility/social-impact programs and budgets
- Credible, low-risk association (not a front group, not adversarial)
- Clear deliverables they can report upward (white papers, coalitions, policy wins)
- Being early to a cause that will become mainstream

**How we speak to them:** Professional, ROI-aware, but mission-driven. We show them that their dollars extend their existing accessibility commitments into civil-rights infrastructure. We never pitch "fund our nonprofit" — we pitch "co-build the policy layer your products need."

**Key message:** "You already fund accessibility. We turn that investment into durable legal and institutional recognition."

### 2. Volunteer / Advocate

**Who:** Disability-rights advocates, neurodivergent self-advocates, legal-aid attorneys, accessibility professionals, students, technologists who care about access.

**What they care about:**
- Authentic disability leadership (not tech companies speaking for them)
- Concrete impact (not more white papers gathering dust)
- Being part of something early and meaningful
- Their expertise being valued, not extracted

**How we speak to them:** Direct, invitational, respectful of lived experience. We acknowledge the tension of corporate funding openly. We position the governance firewall as non-negotiable. We ask for their voice, not just their labor.

**Key message:** "This is your movement. Corporate money funds the work. You govern the mission."

### 3. The General Public

**Who:** Social media audiences, journalists, policymakers, educators, healthcare workers, anyone who encounters AI accessibility in daily life.

**What they care about:**
- Human stories (not policy abstractions)
- Understanding why this matters to them or people they know
- Sharing something that makes them look informed and compassionate

**How we speak to them:** Storytelling-first. Short. Visual. Shareable. We make the case in 15 seconds (social) and 2 minutes (blog). We always include a real scenario.

**Key message:** "Imagine you can't understand your own medical diagnosis — and the tool that could help you is blocked."

### 4. Policymakers / Institutions

**Who:** DOJ, HHS, ED officials, state AGs, university administrators, hospital compliance officers, HR departments.

**What they care about:**
- Legal defensibility
- Precedent and guidance
- Not being the first to act (or the last)
- Clear, implementable standards

**How we speak to them:** Formal, evidence-based, footnoted. We provide model language they can adopt. We reduce their risk by showing that inaction is also a risk.

**Key message:** "Existing law already supports this. We provide the roadmap to implement it."

## Messaging Pillars

### Pillar 1: This is a civil-rights issue, not a tech issue

AI comprehension tools are the modern equivalent of screen readers, sign-language interpreters, and Braille displays. Blocking them in high-stakes settings is an accessibility barrier. The legal framework already exists — we just need institutions to apply it.

### Pillar 2: People are already being harmed by the gap

Real people with cognitive disabilities, reading disabilities, neurodivergent conditions, and communication barriers are being denied the AI tools they need to understand contracts, medical information, benefits notices, and legal documents. This is happening now, not hypothetically.

### Pillar 3: This is good for everyone — not just people with disabilities

Plain-language AI tools help ESL speakers, elderly users, overwhelmed parents, and anyone navigating complex systems. Universal design benefits everyone. Sponsors who fund this are investing in a larger addressable market for accessible AI.

### Pillar 4: We are narrowly focused and legally grounded

We are not asking for general AI rights or agent autonomy. We are asking for one specific thing: recognition that user-authorized AI comprehension tools are legitimate auxiliary aids under existing effective-communication law. This is the most defensible, most socially valuable, and most achievable first step.

## Visual Identity Direction

**Color palette:**
- Primary: Deep blue (trust, authority, accessibility) — suggest #1E3A5F
- Accent: Warm amber/gold (human warmth, urgency) — suggest #D4A017
- Neutral: Warm gray — suggest #4A4A4A
- Background: Off-white — suggest #FAFAF7
- Accessibility green for success/progress states — suggest #2D6A4F

**Typography:**
- Headlines: A clean, modern sans-serif with strong weight contrast (e.g., Inter, Outfit, or Satoshi)
- Body: Highly readable at small sizes, generous line height. Consider 18px base for web.
- Code/data: Monospace for any legal citations or technical references

**Logo direction:**
- Should convey: connection, comprehension, access
- Avoid: generic globe/handshake/puzzle-piece iconography
- Consider: stylized "bridge" motif (bridging law and technology), overlapping speech/thought bubbles, or an abstract "key" representing access
- Must work at 16px favicon size and on dark backgrounds

**Photography/illustration style:**
- Real people in real settings (doctor's office, school, government office)
- Diverse, disability-inclusive, not inspiration-porn
- Warm lighting, natural environments
- Avoid stock-photo sterility

## Content Tone by Channel

| Channel | Tone | Length | Example |
|:--|:--|:--|:--|
| Website homepage | Authoritative, invitational | 200 words above fold | "AI helps millions understand the world. The law hasn't caught up." |
| Sponsor deck | Professional, ROI-focused | 10-12 slides | "Your accessibility investment, amplified." |
| Social media | Human, urgent, shareable | 50-150 words per post | Story-driven scenarios with clear calls to action |
| Blog/newsletter | Explanatory, specific | 500-1000 words | Deep dives on specific settings (healthcare, education) |
| Policy briefs | Formal, footnoted | 2000-5000 words | Legal analysis with model language |
| Email outreach | Warm, concise, action-oriented | 150-300 words | One clear ask per email |

## Naming Conventions

- The organization is **AI Access Alliance** or **AI-AA** (never "the AIAA" — that's the aerospace group)
- The initiative / first campaign is the **Comprehension Access Campaign**
- The legal framework we advocate for is **AI-mediated effective communication**
- The tools we protect are **AI comprehension aids** or **AI accessibility tools**
- The people we serve are **users** or **people who rely on AI for comprehension** (never "the disabled" or "AI users" generically)

## What We Are Not

- We are not an AI industry lobby
- We are not advocating for bot rights or autonomous agent access
- We are not asking companies to open their APIs or data
- We are not a legal defense fund (yet)
- We are not speaking for the disability community — we are building a platform for the community to speak
