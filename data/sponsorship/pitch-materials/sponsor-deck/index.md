#### Slide 2 — Microsoft Variant

**"What Immersive Reader did was give me the ability to be on the same level as everyone else."**

Elle-Mae Taylor was diagnosed with dyslexia as a child. Educators told her to focus on sports instead of academics. She had messy handwriting and couldn't express her thoughts in timed written exams. Then she discovered Microsoft Immersive Reader — page color overlays, line focus, Read Aloud, and dictation gave her tools to comprehend text at the same pace as her peers.

Result: Elle-Mae earned the highest possible marks on her A-Levels. She is now a Microsoft intern who still uses Immersive Reader daily.

**The connection to AI-AA:** If Immersive Reader is an auxiliary aid that transforms academic outcomes for students with dyslexia, then blocking it — or blocking the AI tools that extend its capabilities — constitutes denial of meaningful access under the ADA. Microsoft's investment in AI accessibility tools needs the policy infrastructure that ensures they're recognized, not blocked.

**Supporting data:** EY survey of 300 employees with disabilities: **85% said Copilot creates a more inclusive workplace**, 68% said it reduced work anxiety, 76% said it helped them thrive. In the UK government's Copilot trial, **neurodiverse employees were 25% more satisfied** than neurotypical peers.

*Source: [Microsoft Education Blog, June 2022](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/education/blog/2022/06/how-immersive-reader-helped-a-student-succeed-in-school-and-life/); [Microsoft Source](https://news.microsoft.com/source/features/ai/how-ai-helps-neurodivergent-professionals-showcase-their-strengths/)*

### Slide 4: Why Now

**This is an accessibility crisis hiding in plain sight.**

- **AI comprehension tools have crossed from novelty to necessity.** A peer-reviewed diary study of adults with ADHD found participants describe ChatGPT as "a little bit of a life raft" for bridging neurotypical-neurodivergent communication gaps (ACM, 2025). A branding consultant with dyslexia reports AI gave him "the confidence to stand alongside his peers" — transforming day-long tasks into manageable work.
- **Institutional AI bans are accelerating with no accommodation exception.** Anti-scraping techniques that block bots also "break screen readers and AI comprehension tools," forcing users with disabilities to "miss out on crucial content" (WebProNews, Dec 2025). Accessibility scholars warn that blanket AI bans in education mean "for some students, AI is not a shortcut to avoid thinking but rather a lifeline to their learning" and "ignoring it is not neutrality — it's negligence" (Inside Higher Ed, Nov 2025).
- **No legal framework distinguishes "AI for convenience" from "AI for accessibility."** Without clear guidance, risk-averse institutions default to blocking everything.
- **The window to shape this is now** — before blanket bans become entrenched institutional policy.

### Slide 5: The Legal Foundation

**Existing law. New application.**

The argument rests on three established legal pillars:

1. **The ADA, Section 504, and Section 1557 already require covered entities to provide auxiliary aids and services** to ensure effective communication with people who have disabilities. (42 U.S.C. § 12182(b)(2)(A)(iii); 28 CFR § 36.303; 29 U.S.C. § 794; 42 U.S.C. § 18116)

2. **The statutory definition of "auxiliary aids and services" is explicitly non-exhaustive and technology-neutral.** Congress designed it to evolve. Screen readers, real-time captioning, and text-to-speech were all once novel technologies that became recognized auxiliary aids.

3. **AI comprehension tools serve the same functional purpose** for people with cognitive, intellectual, reading-related, and neurodevelopmental disabilities as screen readers serve for blind users: they transform text into a format the user can meaningfully comprehend.

**Therefore:** When a covered entity blocks a person's authorized AI comprehension tool from accessing text the person is already entitled to read, that entity may be failing its effective-communication obligation under existing law.

Visual timeline:
```
1990: ADA signed → auxiliary aids principle established
1996: Screen readers recognized as auxiliary aids
2010: Video captioning mandated (CVAA)
2016: Web accessibility recognized (Section 508 refresh, WCAG adoption)
202X: AI comprehension tools → THE NEXT RECOGNITION
```

Each followed the same pattern: technology emerged → users adopted → institutions resisted → legal recognition followed → adoption accelerated.

> **Design note:** This slide establishes legal credibility. Use the timeline to show AI comprehension tools as the natural next step in a 35-year trajectory. This is NOT new law — it is new application of existing law.

### Slide 11: Governance & Independence

**Mission-driven. Firewalled. Disability-led.**

The most important slide for addressing the "industry lobby" concern:

- Board and advisory council are **disability-led** — majority representation
- Sponsor agreements **expressly deny** funders control over:
  - Legal positions and enforcement targets
  - Policy recommendations
  - Research findings
  - Hiring and personnel decisions
  - Pre-publication review (except 48-hour factual accuracy check)
- Sponsors receive **advisory seats only** — non-voting, with mandatory recusal on conflicts
- All policy positions developed independently
- All sponsors and tier levels publicly disclosed

"AI-AA is a disability-rights organization that happens to align with responsible industry interests — not the other way around."

> **Design note:** Use the governance firewall as a trust signal. This slide should feel like a guarantee, not a disclaimer.

### A3: Comparable Precedents

How prior auxiliary aid categories gained recognition — each followed the same four-stage pattern:

| Technology | Emerged | Adopted | Resisted | Recognized | Result |
|-----------|---------|---------|----------|------------|--------|
| **Screen readers** | 1980s (JAWS 1995) | Blind users adopted for computer access | Websites built without alt text, inaccessible PDFs | Section 508 refresh, court rulings (Target, Winn-Dixie) | Now standard in every OS, required by law |
| **Video captioning** | 1970s (open captions) | Deaf/HoH users, then mainstream | Broadcasters resisted cost; web video uncaptioned | CVAA 2010 mandated online captioning | 80%+ of social video viewers use captions today |
| **Web accessibility** | WCAG 1.0 (1999) | Users with diverse disabilities | Most websites non-compliant; "too expensive" arguments | Section 508 refresh (2017), DOJ guidance, 4,000+ lawsuits/year | $13B+ accessibility tech market |
| **Real-time captioning** | CART (1980s) | Students, employees who are deaf/HoH | Schools resisted cost of CART providers | IDEA, Section 504 case law, ADA Title II | Standard accommodation in education and employment |
| **AI comprehension tools** | 2022-present | Millions of users with cognitive/reading disabilities | Schools, hospitals, government blocking AI access | **← WE ARE HERE** | ? |

The pattern is clear. AI-AA accelerates the recognition phase — moving from "institutions resist" to "legal clarity enables adoption."
