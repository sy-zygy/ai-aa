## Email 1: Initial Outreach to Jenny Lay-Flurrie, Chief Accessibility Officer

**To:** Jenny Lay-Flurrie, VP & Chief Accessibility Officer, Microsoft
**From:** [CEO Name], CEO, AI Access Alliance
**Subject:** AI comprehension tools as auxiliary aids — a gap your work can help close

---

Jenny,

I'm writing because Microsoft's accessibility work — particularly AI for Accessibility and Immersive Reader — is the best evidence I've seen that AI tools have become essential comprehension aids for people with cognitive and reading-related disabilities.

Elle-Mae Taylor was told to focus on sports instead of academics because of her dyslexia. Then she found Immersive Reader. She earned the highest possible marks on her A-Levels and is now a Microsoft intern. Her words: "What Immersive Reader did was give me the ability to be on the same level as everyone else." Your EY study found 85% of employees with disabilities say Copilot creates a more inclusive workplace. Immersive Reader has 35 million monthly users. These aren't hypothetical tools — they're changing outcomes for real people.

But there's a gap. When a hospital deploys anti-bot protections on its patient portal, or a school implements a blanket AI ban without an accommodation exception, these tools stop working — in exactly the settings where comprehension matters most. There is currently no legal or policy framework that distinguishes "AI for convenience" from "AI for accessibility."

**AI Access Alliance** is a new 501(c)(3) building that framework. Our core argument: AI comprehension tools serve the same function for cognitive and reading-related disabilities that screen readers serve for blindness — and existing law (ADA effective-communication, auxiliary-aids provisions) already supports recognizing them. We're not pushing new legislation. We're applying established principles to the next category of assistive technology, following the same pattern as screen readers, captioning, and web accessibility before them.

I'd welcome 20 minutes to share what we're building and hear your perspective on where the institutional barriers are worst for Microsoft's accessibility products. No pitch — just a conversation about a problem I suspect you've seen firsthand.

I'll also be at the Ability Summit May 19-20 and would be glad to connect in person if that's easier.

Best,
[CEO Name]
CEO, AI Access Alliance
ai-access-alliance.org

Microsoft Elevate's commitment to K-12 schools, community colleges, and nonprofits is exactly the ecosystem where AI comprehension tools matter most — and where institutional barriers block them.

AI Access Alliance is a new 501(c)(3) working to get AI comprehension tools recognized as auxiliary aids under existing disability law (ADA, Section 504). But without legal and institutional recognition, schools and hospitals default to blocking everything.

I'd value a conversation about how AI Access Alliance might align with Elevate's mission. We're not asking Microsoft to start something new — we're asking you to extend an existing commitment into the policy layer that makes it durable.
