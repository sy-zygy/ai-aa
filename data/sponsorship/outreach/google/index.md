## Email 1: Initial Outreach to Eve Andersson, Senior Director, Products for All

**To:** Eve Andersson, Senior Director, Products for All, Google
**From:** [CEO Name], CEO, AI Access Alliance
**Subject:** The policy gap between building accessibility tools and getting them recognized

---

Eve,

Google's accessibility work sets the standard — Read Along has improved reading fluency by 81% in studies. TalkBack with Gemini gives blind users the ability to ask follow-up questions about images they encounter 90+ times per day. Expressive Captions capture not just words but tone and emotion. These aren't experimental features — they're daily infrastructure for millions of people with disabilities.

But there's a gap between building these tools and getting them recognized. When a school district implements a blanket AI ban, it doesn't distinguish Read Along from a general chatbot. When a government agency adds bot-blocking measures to its benefits portal, Select-to-Speak and Chrome's Read Aloud stop working. The tools exist — but institutions have no framework for when AI-powered accessibility is required, not optional.

**AI Access Alliance** is a new 501(c)(3) building that framework. Our core argument: AI comprehension tools serve the same function for cognitive and reading-related disabilities that screen readers serve for blindness — and existing law (ADA effective-communication, auxiliary-aids provisions) already supports recognizing them. We're applying established legal principles, not pushing new legislation. The recognition pattern is identical to screen readers, captioning, and web accessibility.

Google's "Through My Eyes" platform with Understood.org shows what it looks like when AI companies partner with disability organizations to create real impact. AI-AA takes that a step further — creating the legal and institutional standards that make your products' accessibility impact durable across healthcare, education, and government.

I'd welcome 20 minutes to share what we're building and hear your perspective on where institutional barriers are blocking Google's accessibility products. No pitch — just a conversation about a problem I suspect your team encounters regularly.

I'll be at events around GAAD (May 21) and would be glad to connect in person if that's easier.

Best,
[CEO Name]
CEO, AI Access Alliance
ai-access-alliance.org

Maggie,

Google.org's AI Opportunity Fund ($75M US, $120M global) is the largest private investment in AI skills training for underserved communities. The Generative AI Accelerator funds organizations building AI solutions that collectively aim to serve 30M+ people. These programs create real capacity — but without legal recognition of AI accessibility tools, that capacity hits a wall in the settings where it matters most.

AI Access Alliance is a new 501(c)(3) working to get AI comprehension tools recognized as auxiliary aids under existing disability law (ADA, Section 504). Google's products — Read Along (81% reading improvement), Gemini summarization in Workspace, Select-to-Speak, Chrome Read Aloud — are the strongest evidence that AI accessibility tools work. But schools, hospitals, and government agencies have no framework for distinguishing these tools from general chatbots. Without policy infrastructure, institutional defaults block everything.

Google.org already partners with Understood.org for neurodivergent students and funds CSEveryone ($1M) and Tech Kids Unlimited ($250K) for disability education. AI-AA operates at the same intersection — but at the policy layer that makes these investments durable.

We're building the doctrine, evidence base, and model institutional policies that create legal clarity for AI accessibility tools. Your AI Opportunity Fund grantees — Goodwill, InnovateUS, Partnership for Public Service — are training people to use AI. AI-AA ensures those people can keep using AI as an accommodation in the regulated settings where they need it most.

Would you be open to 20 minutes to explore alignment?
