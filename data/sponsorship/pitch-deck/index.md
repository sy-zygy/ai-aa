## Slide 5: The Legal Foundation

# Existing law. New application.

The argument rests on three established legal pillars:

**1. Existing obligation.** The ADA, Section 504, and Section 1557 already require covered entities to provide auxiliary aids and services for effective communication. (42 U.S.C. 12182; 28 CFR 36.303; 29 U.S.C. 794; 42 U.S.C. 18116)

**2. Technology-neutral definition.** Congress designed "auxiliary aids and services" to evolve. The statutory definition is explicitly non-exhaustive. Screen readers, captioning, and text-to-speech were all once novel technologies that became recognized auxiliary aids.

**3. Same function, new technology.** AI comprehension tools serve the same functional purpose for people with cognitive, intellectual, reading-related, and neurodevelopmental disabilities as screen readers serve for blind users: they transform text into a format the user can meaningfully comprehend.

**Therefore:** When a covered entity blocks a person's authorized AI comprehension tool from accessing text the person is already entitled to read, that entity may be failing its effective-communication obligation under existing law.

### The pattern repeats:

```
1990  ADA signed .................. auxiliary aids principle established
1996  Screen readers .............. recognized as auxiliary aids
2010  Video captioning ............ mandated (CVAA)
2016  Web accessibility ........... recognized (Section 508 refresh)
202X  AI comprehension tools ...... THE NEXT RECOGNITION ← WE ARE HERE
```

Each followed the same arc: technology emerged, users adopted, institutions resisted, legal recognition followed, adoption accelerated.

## Appendix B: Objection Responses

**"This sounds like an industry lobby group."**
Our governance model prevents it. Board and advisory council are disability-led. Sponsor agreements expressly deny funders control over legal positions. We publish our positions when the public sees them — no sponsor preview. The governance firewall is incorporated into our bylaws and can only be amended by 2/3 board vote.

**"Why fund advocacy that might create compliance obligations?"**
AI-AA creates clarity, not liability. Our doctrine says institutions should say *yes* to AI comprehension tools under clear conditions. That expands your market — institutions currently blocking AI tools would adopt them. Market expansion, not regulation.

**"We already fund accessibility work."**
Without legal recognition, the tools you fund are optional conveniences in regulated settings. When a hospital blocks AI, your accessibility investment doesn't help the patient. AI-AA removes the institutional barriers so your products work where stakes are highest.

**"It's too early / unproven legal theory."**
Based on existing law — ADA effective-communication, auxiliary-aids provisions. Not new law, new application. Every prior auxiliary aid (screen readers, captioning, web accessibility) followed the same pattern. Your own products are the evidence.

**"What about AI risks in high-stakes settings?"**
Our Corporate Standards workstream addresses exactly this — model policies for "allowable AI comprehension" with trust-and-safety guardrails. We're not arguing for all AI everywhere. We're arguing for user-authorized comprehension tools with safeguards, in settings where the alternative is no comprehension at all.
