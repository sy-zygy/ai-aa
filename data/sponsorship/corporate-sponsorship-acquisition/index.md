# Corporate Sponsorship Acquisition

**Owner:** Sponsorships Director | **Status:** Active | **Updated:** 2026-04-05

The canonical playbook for acquiring corporate sponsors. Every outreach interaction, from first research to signed agreement, follows this sequence.

---

## The Outreach Sequence

### Stage 1: Research (2-3 weeks per target)

**Goal:** Know the target's accessibility investments better than they do.

**Deliverables:**
- Complete sponsor profile (see [[Sponsor Profiles]] template)
- Specific accessibility programs with dollar amounts and program leads
- Products that function as comprehension aids
- Leadership and decision-maker mapping
- Budget lines AI-AA aligns with
- Anticipated objections and pre-drafted responses
- Warm introduction pathways identified

**Exit criteria:** Profile is complete and reviewed. You can explain in 60 seconds why this company should fund AI-AA and which existing budget line covers it.

### Stage 2: Warm Introduction (1-2 weeks)

**Goal:** Get introduced to the right person by someone they trust.

One warm intro is worth 50 cold emails. Prioritize these channels:

1. **Be My Eyes** — Mike Buckley (CEO) connects to Microsoft, OpenAI, and Meta. Highest-value single bridge relationship.
2. **Disability:IN** — All 5 Tier 1 targets are corporate partners. Conference networking is a prime opportunity.
3. **Partnership on AI (PAI)** — All targets are PAI partners. AI governance events create natural overlap.
4. **Conference encounters** — Microsoft Ability Summit (May 19-20), GAAD (May 21), AI Governance Summit (May 20, Seattle), Disability:IN Global Conference (Summer 2026).
5. **Advisory council connections** — As advisory council forms, map member connections to target companies.
6. **Direct cold outreach** — Last resort. Only after warm paths are exhausted. Target accessibility teams, not generic CSR.

**Template: Warm Introduction Request**

> [Mutual Contact],
>
> I'm reaching out because [reason for connection]. I lead sponsorship for AI Access Alliance, a new disability-rights nonprofit focused on getting AI comprehension tools recognized as auxiliary aids under existing disability law (ADA, Section 504).
>
> I noticed that [Target Company]'s [Specific Program/Product] is exactly the kind of tool our work protects. I'd love a brief introduction to [Specific Person] to explore alignment. Would you be open to connecting us?
>
> Happy to send a one-page summary in advance so they have context.

### Stage 3: Initial Outreach (1 week)

**Goal:** Secure a 20-minute conversation. Not a pitch — a conversation.

**Rules:**
- Reference their specific program or product. Never send generic outreach.
- Ask for time, not money. "Would you have 20 minutes to discuss how our work connects to [specific program]?"
- Attach the one-page Executive Summary (see [[Executive Summary]]).
- Keep the email under 200 words.
- If you have a warm intro, use their name in the subject line.

**Template: Initial Outreach Email**

> Subject: [Mutual Contact] suggested I reach out — AI accessibility & [Company Product]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> [Mutual Contact] suggested we connect. I'm [Name], leading sponsorships for AI Access Alliance — a new disability-rights nonprofit.
>
> We're working to get AI comprehension tools recognized as auxiliary aids under existing disability law. Your team's work on [Specific Product/Program] is exactly what our legal framework protects — [one sentence connecting their product to the mission].
>
> I'd love 20 minutes to share what we're building and hear how it connects to [Company]'s accessibility strategy.
>
> I've attached a one-page summary. Would any time next week work?
>
> Best,
> [Name]

### Stage 4: Sponsor Conversation (30-60 minutes)

**Goal:** Establish mission alignment and understand their decision process.

**Structure:**
1. **Listen first** (5 min) — Ask about their accessibility strategy and what matters to them. Let them talk.
2. **Tell a story** (5 min) — One concrete harm story. A person with dyslexia whose hospital blocked their AI tool. A blind student whose university banned the comprehension aid they depend on. Make it specific, not abstract.
3. **Present the mission** (10 min) — What AI-AA does, the legal foundation, the four workstreams, year-one milestones. Use the sponsor deck.
4. **Show the fit** (5 min) — Explain exactly how their products connect. Use the customized "Why [Company]" slide.
5. **Governance** (5 min) — Proactively explain the firewall. They will ask. Be first.
6. **The ask** (5 min) — "We're building a coalition of founding sponsors at the $500K-$1M level. The companies that commit now shape the framework. Would [Company] want to be at the table?"
7. **Next steps** (5 min) — Identify internal champion, decision-maker, budget authority, and timeline. Schedule follow-up.

**Rules:**
- Never pitch money in the first meeting unless they bring it up first.
- If they bring up objections, welcome them. Every objection is a signal of interest.
- Take notes on everything — what they care about, who else they mention, what concerns them. Log in the sponsor profile.
- Send a follow-up email within 24 hours with a thank-you, summary of key points, and proposed next step.

### Stage 5: Follow-Up Package (within 1 week of conversation)

**Goal:** Give them everything they need to champion AI-AA internally.

**Send:**
1. One-page Executive Summary (tailored to their context)
2. Customized sponsor tier summary with their specific benefits
3. 2-3 relevant user stories (featuring their products if possible)
4. Advisory council member list (as it develops)
5. Suggested next step — "Would it be helpful if I spoke with [internal champion they mentioned]?"

**Rules:**
- Every document should be customized. Their company name, product references, and relevant sector examples should appear throughout.
- Include the governance firewall language prominently. They will share internally and legal will review.
- Don't overwhelm. 5 pages max across all materials. Quality over quantity.

### Stage 6: Deep Dive (if requested)

**Goal:** Address detailed questions from their legal, CSR, or policy teams.

This stage happens when the target is seriously interested and needs to socialize the commitment internally. Expect questions about:

- Legal structure and 501(c)(3) status (see Operations guidance on fiscal sponsorship bridge)
- Governance firewall mechanics — how exactly are sponsors prevented from influencing positions?
- Conflict of interest provisions
- How sponsorship funds are used (budget allocation)
- Other sponsors in the coalition (who else is committed or in conversation)
- Publication and research independence
- Brand association risks

**Provide:** Full proposal (when available), draft sponsor agreement, governance policy excerpts, conflict of interest policy.

### Stage 7: Close (timeline varies)

**Goal:** Signed agreement, internal champion confirmed, advisory council nomination, press announcement coordinated.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Sponsorship agreement signed (must include governance firewall provisions — see [[Sponsor Governance Policy]])
- [ ] Operations review of agreement completed
- [ ] Logo and brand assets received for website/materials
- [ ] Advisory council representative nominated (Founding tier)
- [ ] Internal champion identified (day-to-day relationship owner)
- [ ] Press announcement coordinated with Communications Director
- [ ] Sponsor onboarded to impact reporting cadence
- [ ] Thank-you and welcome communication sent

## Objection Handling

Pre-drafted responses to the objections you will encounter in every conversation.

### "This sounds like an industry lobby group."

> "That's the most important objection you could raise, and it's the one we've designed our entire governance structure around. Our board is disability-led. Our advisory council will be majority disabled and neurodivergent leaders. Our sponsor agreements expressly deny funders any control over legal positions, policy recommendations, or research conclusions. We publicly disclose all sponsor names and tiers. We built the firewall first — before the first pitch — because if it's ever perceived as compromised, the mission is dead."

### "Why fund advocacy that might create compliance obligations?"

> "We're not creating new obligations — we're creating clarity about existing ones. The ADA already requires effective communication and auxiliary aids. AI tools already function as auxiliary aids for millions of people. What's missing is formal recognition. When a hospital blocks AI on its patient portal, they're already creating a potential ADA problem — they just don't know it yet. Our work creates the framework that says 'yes' to AI tools under clear conditions. That expands the market for your products in institutional settings."

### "We already fund accessibility work."

> "And your accessibility investments are exactly why we're here. But without legal and institutional recognition, the tools you fund hit a wall. A hospital can block Copilot on its patient portal. A school can ban ChatGPT without an accommodation exception. A court can refuse AI-assisted plain-language explanations. Your tools work brilliantly — until an institution says no. AI-AA removes the 'no' by establishing that user-authorized AI comprehension tools are protected auxiliary aids under existing law."

### "It's too early / unproven legal theory."

> "The legal foundation isn't new law — it's existing law applied to new technology. The ADA requires effective communication. The regulations define auxiliary aids in technology-neutral terms that were specifically designed to accommodate technological evolution. Screen readers, captioning, speech-to-text — all followed the same path: new technology, existing law, formal recognition. AI comprehension tools are next. Early sponsors shape how the framework develops."

### "What about AI risks in high-stakes settings?"

> "We're not arguing for all AI everywhere. We're arguing for user-authorized comprehension tools with appropriate safeguards. Our trust-and-safety workstream specifically addresses institutional deployment standards — when and how AI tools should be available in healthcare, education, legal, and government settings. The framework includes guardrails. Sponsors who join early help shape what those guardrails look like."

### "Governance concerns — what if you take a position we disagree with?"

> "That will happen. Our governance firewall exists specifically for that moment. Your sponsorship agreement expressly states that AI-AA retains sole authority over its policy positions. You will not receive advance review of our legal filings or research publications. If we take a position your company disagrees with, that's evidence the firewall works — and that's what gives AI-AA credibility with the disability community, policymakers, and the press."

## Pipeline Management

### Pipeline Stages

| Stage | Definition | Duration |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| **Research** | Building sponsor profile, no contact yet | 2-3 weeks |
| **Warm Intro** | Seeking introduction path | 1-2 weeks |
| **Outreach** | Initial contact made, awaiting response | 1-2 weeks |
| **Conversation** | First meeting held or scheduled | 1-2 weeks |
| **Follow-Up** | Post-meeting materials sent, awaiting internal review | 2-4 weeks |
| **Deep Dive** | Detailed questions from their team, proposal shared | 2-6 weeks |
| **Negotiation** | Agreement terms under discussion | 2-4 weeks |
| **Closed** | Agreement signed | — |

### Pipeline Rules

1. **Update after every interaction.** Every email, call, meeting, or internal discussion should be logged in the sponsor profile's Outreach History section with date, action, outcome, and next step.
2. **Every prospect has a next action.** If a prospect doesn't have a clear next action with an owner and deadline, it's stalled. Escalate stalled prospects to CEO.
3. **Never let warm leads cool.** If someone expresses interest, respond within 24 hours. Schedule the next interaction before ending the current one.
4. **Multi-thread relationships.** Don't rely on a single contact. Identify at least two people at each target — an internal champion and a decision-maker.
5. **Track budget cycles.** Corporate sponsorships are often tied to fiscal year budgets. Know when your target's fiscal year starts and plan your close timeline accordingly.

## Governance Firewall — Contract Language

Every sponsorship agreement must include these provisions (from [[Sponsor Governance Policy]], Section 5):

> **Governance Independence.** Sponsor acknowledges and agrees that this sponsorship does not confer any voting rights, veto authority, or governance role within AI Access Alliance. AI-AA retains sole and exclusive authority over its policy positions, legal filings, research publications, enforcement decisions, hiring, partnerships, and all other organizational decisions. Sponsor shall not condition any portion of this sponsorship on AI-AA taking or refraining from taking any specific position or action.

> **No Pre-Publication Review.** Sponsor acknowledges that it will not receive advance access to AI-AA's policy positions, research publications, or legal filings prior to public release, except for limited factual accuracy checks as described in the Sponsor Governance Policy.

> **Public Disclosure.** AI-AA will publicly disclose the names and sponsorship tiers of all Sponsors. Specific financial amounts may be disclosed in aggregate or individually, at AI-AA's discretion.

All agreements are reviewed by Operations before execution.
