# Operations

**Owner:** Operations Director | **Status:** Active | **Updated:** 2026-04-05

The Operations department builds and maintains the organizational infrastructure that turns AI Access Alliance from a mission statement into a functioning, credible non-profit. Governance, legal entity, board, advisory council, compliance, and operational processes — if it keeps the organization running and accountable, it lives here.

## Department Scope

### What's In Scope

- **Non-profit formation** — 501(c)(3) filing, articles of incorporation, bylaws, EIN, state registrations
- **Governance design** — Board structure, advisory council structure, sponsor governance firewall
- **Board recruitment** — Identifying, vetting, and onboarding board members
- **Advisory council coordination** — Working with Research Director to recruit expert advisors
- **Governance documents** — Bylaws, conflict-of-interest policy, whistleblower policy, document retention, board member agreements
- **Compliance** — IRS Form 990 filing, state registration renewals, grant reporting requirements
- **Financial infrastructure** — Bank accounts, accounting systems, fiscal policies, budget tracking
- **Organizational development** — Team structure, operating processes, internal coordination

### What's Out of Scope

- Strategic direction (that's the CEO)
- Legal doctrine or policy work (that's Policy)
- Fundraising and sponsor relationships (that's Development — we review governance provisions)
- Public communications (that's Comms)
- Research and evidence (that's Research — we coordinate on advisory council)

## Mission Contribution

Operations is the credibility infrastructure:

| Department | What They Need From Us |
|------------|----------------------|
| **CEO** | A well-governed organization to lead. Board and advisory council that reflect the mission. Processes that let the team move fast without compliance risk. |
| **Development** | Sponsor agreements with governance firewall provisions. A governance structure that sponsors (and their critics) can trust. 501(c)(3) status for tax-deductible contributions. |
| **Policy** | Conflict-of-interest policies that specifically address the tension between sponsor interests and policy positions. Organizational credibility that backs the legal argument. |
| **Communications** | Board and advisory council names that signal legitimacy. Governance story that answers "industry front" criticism. |
| **Research** | Advisory council infrastructure for expert recruitment and engagement. |

## The Governance Imperative

This department's most critical responsibility is the **governance firewall** between sponsor money and mission direction:

- The board must include disabled and neurodivergent leaders — not just AI industry representatives
- Sponsor agreements must expressly deny funders control over legal positions, enforcement targets, or policy recommendations
- The advisory council must have non-voting sponsor seats clearly separated from mission-governing seats
- The conflict-of-interest policy must specifically address situations where policy positions affect sponsor interests

**The test:** "Would a disability-rights journalist writing a critical profile find anything to exploit in our governance structure?"

If the answer isn't a confident "no," the governance structure needs work.

## Entity Structure Decision

**Recommended:** 501(c)(3) with a future 501(c)(4) affiliate.

- **501(c)(3) now** — Tax-exempt. Allows tax-deductible donations. Best for receiving corporate sponsorships and foundation grants. Restriction: limited lobbying activity.
- **501(c)(4) later** — If direct lobbying becomes necessary. No tax-deductible donations, but unlimited lobbying. Most disability-rights organizations use this dual structure.

## Operating Model

### How This Department Works

- **Process creates freedom.** Good governance structures let the team move fast without worrying about compliance.
- **Draft early, iterate.** Get governance documents to 80% quickly, refine with board feedback.
- **Template from the best.** Study governance structures of DREDF, AAPD, Center for Democracy and Technology. Don't reinvent — adapt proven models.
- **Coordinate with CEO.** Board recruitment is shared. CEO provides strategic direction on who; Operations executes the pipeline.
- **Protect the firewall.** Review every sponsor-facing governance provision. If it could be interpreted as giving sponsors influence, rewrite it.

### Board Recruitment Process

1. **Research** — Identify candidates from disability-rights orgs, law schools, accessibility conferences, policy institutes
2. **Profile** — Background, expertise, alignment, connections, potential concerns
3. **Vet** — Conflicts of interest, public positions, controversies
4. **Approach** — Warm introduction, mission brief, board commitment overview
5. **Interview** — Mission alignment, time commitment, governance expectations
6. **Appoint** — Board vote (once initial board is seated), onboarding

### Priority Candidate Sources

- National Council on Disability
- Disability Rights Education and Defense Fund (DREDF)
- American Association of People with Disabilities (AAPD)
- Center for Democracy and Technology (accessibility program)
- Law school disability law clinics (Harvard, Georgetown, Berkeley, NYU)
- Assistive technology organizations (ATIA, RESNA)
- Self-advocacy organizations (ASAN, SABE, The Arc) — approach carefully given ASAN's AI skepticism

### Cross-Department Coordination

- **With Development:** Review all sponsor agreements for governance firewall provisions. Ensure sponsor tier benefits don't inadvertently grant policy influence.
- **With Policy:** Conflict-of-interest policy must address sponsor-interest/policy-position tensions. Coordinate on legal entity decisions.
- **With Research:** Joint effort on advisory council recruitment. Research identifies expert candidates; Operations manages the pipeline.
- **With Communications:** Governance story supports "not an industry front" narrative. Board/advisory announcements coordinated with Comms.
