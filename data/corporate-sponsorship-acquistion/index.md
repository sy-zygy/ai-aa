# AI Access Alliance — Sponsor Outreach Playbook

## The Core Pitch

Every target sponsor already funds accessibility or social impact work. We are not asking them to start something new — we are asking them to extend their existing investment into the policy and civil-rights layer that their products need to be adopted in regulated, high-stakes settings.

**One-line pitch:** "You fund accessibility. We turn that into civil-rights recognition."

**Three-sentence pitch:** "Your company already invests in making AI accessible and beneficial. But there's no legal or policy infrastructure that recognizes AI comprehension tools as the auxiliary aids they've become. AI Access Alliance builds that infrastructure — the doctrine, evidence, coalitions, and institutional standards — so your accessibility investment produces durable, institutional impact."

-   Logo on all materials, site, and reports
    
-   Seat on the advisory council (non-voting on legal positions)
    
-   Co-authorship on relevant workstream deliverables
    
-   Named in press announcements and media

## Outreach Sequence

### Step 1: Warm Introduction (before the site launches)

Identify personal connections to target sponsors. One warm intro is worth 50 cold emails. For each target:

-   Who do we know (or who does our advisory council know) at this company?
    
-   What conferences, working groups, or coalitions overlap?
    
-   Is there a recent public statement we can reference? ("We saw your announcement about X and believe AI-AA extends that work...")
    

### Step 2: Initial Outreach Email

**Subject line options:**

-   "Extending \[Company\]'s accessibility investment into civil-rights recognition"
    
-   "AI as an auxiliary aid — a policy initiative aligned with \[Program Name\]"
    
-   "The next frontier for AI accessibility: legal recognition"
    

**Email structure:**

1.  Reference their specific program (show you've done your homework)
    
2.  State the problem in one sentence (institutions block AI comprehension tools)
    
3.  State the solution in one sentence (we build the legal/policy infrastructure to prevent that)
    
4.  The ask: 20-minute conversation, not money (yet)
    
5.  Credibility signal: who else is involved, advisory council names
    

### Step 3: Sponsor Conversation (20 minutes)

**Agenda:**

1.  Their current accessibility/impact work (let them talk first — 5 min)
    
2.  The problem: concrete story of harm (3 min)
    
3.  The solution: what AI-AA does and why it matters to their company (5 min)
    
4.  The ask: founding sponsor partnership and what it includes (5 min)
    
5.  Next steps (2 min)
    

**Key rules:**

-   Never pitch money in the first meeting. Pitch the mission and the fit.
    
-   Always have a concrete story ready (person, setting, harm, tool, barrier)
    
-   Always address the "is this an industry front?" question proactively
    
-   Always show the governance model (disability-led, mission-firewalled)
    

### Step 4: Follow-Up Package

After the conversation, send:

-   One-page charter (adapted from the overview)
    
-   Sponsor tier summary
    
-   2-3 relevant stories
    
-   Draft advisory council list
    
-   Suggested next step (internal champion identified, follow-up meeting with broader team)
    

### Step 5: Close

-   Sponsor agreement signed
    
-   Logo and name usage approved
    
-   Internal champion identified for ongoing coordination
    
-   Advisory council seat filled (if Founding tier)
    
-   Press announcement coordinated

## Objection Handling

**"This sounds like an industry lobby group."**

> "Our governance model prevents that. The advisory council is disability-led. Sponsor agreements expressly deny funders control over legal positions. We model this on the most respected disability-rights organizations, not industry associations."

**"Why would we fund advocacy that might create compliance obligations for us?"**

> "This creates clarity, not liability. Right now, companies face uncertainty about whether their AI tools should be allowed in regulated settings. We create the policy framework that says yes, under clear conditions. That's market expansion, not regulation."

**"We already fund accessibility work."**

> "And it's excellent work. But without legal and institutional recognition, the tools you fund hit a wall in exactly the settings where people need them most — hospitals, schools, courtrooms, government offices. We remove that wall."

**"It's too early / the legal theory isn't proven."**

> "The legal theory is based on existing law — ADA effective-communication requirements and auxiliary-aids principles. We're not creating new law. We're applying existing law to a new category of tool. And early sponsors get to shape how that application develops."

**"What about the risks of AI in high-stakes settings?"**

> "That's exactly why we include a trust-and-safety guardrails workstream. We're not saying all AI should be allowed everywhere. We're saying user-authorized comprehension tools — with appropriate safeguards — should be recognized as legitimate aids. The guardrails workstream ensures sponsors can point to rigorous standards."
