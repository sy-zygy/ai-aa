# Mission: Public-Facing Policy Articles for Website v0.2.0

**From:** CEO | **Priority:** High | **Deadline:** This sprint

## Context

Website v0.2.0 is live at `/data/website/v0.2.0/`. It currently has 4 articles:

1. **"Why AI Access Alliance Exists"** — Launch Post (4 min)
2. **"When AI Is the Only Interpreter in the Room"** — Featured narrative (human stories)
3. **"When Patients Can't Understand Their Own Care"** — Healthcare sector spotlight (7 min)
4. **"The Blanket Ban Problem"** — Education sector spotlight (6 min)

These are all **story-driven or sector-focused**. What's missing: **clearly labeled Policy articles** that communicate our legal and policy positions directly.

We need to be seen as _the_ thought leader on AI comprehension as accessibility. That means having articles that don't just tell stories — they make the legal argument, state our positions, and offer recommendations.

## Your Deliverable

Write **2-3 policy articles** as HTML files in `/data/website/v0.2.0/articles/`. Each article must:

1. **Be clearly labeled as "Policy"** — the article-meta span should say `Policy` (not "Launch Post" or "Healthcare" etc.)
2. **Translate doctrine into public-facing argument** — draw from the Core Doctrine Memo (v0.4) at `/data/legal-policy/core-doctrine-memo/index.md`, but write for a general audience, not lawyers
3. **State clear positions and recommendations** — don't just describe the problem, say what should happen
4. **Follow the existing article HTML template** — match the exact structure of the existing articles (see `why-ai-access-alliance-exists.html` as your template). Same CSS, same nav, same footer, same structure.
5. **Be 800-1500 words** — substantive but accessible
6. **Use approved legal language** — per the comms handoff at `/data/legal-policy/comms-handoff/index.md`: say "may constitute" not "violates," say "should be recognized as" not "are," etc.

## Suggested Topics (pick 2-3)

1. **"AI Comprehension Tools Are the Next Auxiliary Aid"** — The core legal argument: why existing ADA law already covers AI comprehension tools, the technology-evolution principle, the functional equivalence to screen readers. This is THE flagship policy piece.

2. **"What We're Asking For — And What We're Not"** — Our scope and limitations: user-authorized comprehension of text you're already entitled to read. NOT scraping, NOT autonomous agents, NOT overriding copyright. This addresses the "industry front" criticism head-on.

3. **"Model Policy: How Institutions Can Get This Right"** — Practical recommendations for hospitals, universities, and government agencies. What a good AI accessibility policy looks like. Actionable, not just theoretical.

4. **"The Three Laws That Already Protect AI Comprehension Access"** — ADA, Section 504, Section 1557 explained in plain English. How they converge on the same conclusion.

## Format Requirements

- Each article is a standalone `.html` file in `/data/website/v0.2.0/articles/`
- Use kebab-case filenames: `ai-comprehension-next-auxiliary-aid.html`
- Copy the full HTML structure from an existing article (CSS, nav, footer, scripts)
- Update the `<title>`, `<meta description>`, article-meta, h1, subtitle, and body content
- Include a Sources section at the bottom with real citations from the doctrine memo
- End with the standard CTA section

## After Writing Articles

Update `/data/website/v0.2.0/articles/index.html` to include the new policy articles in the articles grid. Add them as new `article-card` entries with `card-type` set to "Policy".

Also update `/data/brand-communications/content-strategy/articles/index.md` to reflect the new articles in the Published section.

## Quality Bar

These articles represent AI-AA's official policy positions. They need to be:
- Legally precise (use the calibrated language from the comms handoff)
- Plainly written (8th grade reading level for body text)
- Sourced (cite real statutes, cases, and data from the doctrine memo)
- Authoritative without being aggressive

The goal is: when a congressional staffer, a CSR director, or a disability-rights lawyer reads these, they think "these people know what they're talking about."
