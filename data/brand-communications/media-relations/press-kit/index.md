# Press Kit — AI Access Alliance

## Mission Summary

**AI Access Alliance** is a disability-rights initiative building the legal, policy, and public infrastructure to ensure AI comprehension tools are recognized as legitimate accessibility accommodations under existing law.

**The problem:** Millions of Americans with cognitive, learning, and neurological disabilities rely on AI tools to understand complex written information — medical instructions, legal documents, benefits notices, educational materials. But there is no institutional framework that recognizes these tools as accessibility aids. Hospitals block AI on patient portals. Universities ban AI without disability exemptions. Government forms remain incomprehensible to the people they serve.

**The legal basis:** The Americans with Disabilities Act already requires "auxiliary aids and services" for effective communication. This technology-neutral category has evolved for decades — from TTY devices to screen readers to captioned video. AI comprehension tools are the next logical step. No new legislation is required. We are asking institutions to apply existing law to modern tools.

**What we do:**
- Build the legal doctrine that positions AI comprehension tools within established disability-rights frameworks
- Document the human evidence — real stories of people who depend on AI to understand critical information
- Convene a coalition of disability-rights organizations, legal experts, accessibility practitioners, and allied institutions
- Advocate for federal agency guidance recognizing AI comprehension tools as a category of auxiliary aid
- Develop model institutional policies for healthcare, education, government, legal, and employment settings

**What we are not:**
- We are not an AI industry lobby
- We are not advocating for bot rights or autonomous agent access
- We are not asking companies to open their APIs or data
- We do not speak for the disability community — we build infrastructure for the community to be heard

- **26% of American adults** read below a 6th-grade level (NAAL)
- **61 million Americans** live with a disability (CDC)
- The average hospital discharge summary is written at a **college reading level**
- The ADA's "auxiliary aids" category was explicitly designed to **evolve with technology**
- The DOJ has repeatedly confirmed that effective-communication obligations apply to **digital platforms and modern technology**
- **Zero federal guidance** currently addresses AI as an accessibility accommodation

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why does this need a separate organization?**
A: Because no one is doing it. Disability-rights organizations focus on established accommodations. AI companies focus on products. Nobody is building the legal and institutional bridge between civil-rights law and AI comprehension tools. That's the gap AI-AA fills.

**Q: Is this funded by AI companies?**
A: AI-AA seeks corporate sponsorship from companies whose products are already used as comprehension aids. Sponsors fund operations but do not govern the mission. AI-AA maintains a governance firewall: research, doctrine, and advocacy positions are set by disability community advisors and experts, not sponsors.

**Q: What about AI reliability? These tools make mistakes.**
A: Every auxiliary aid has limitations. Human interpreters make errors. Screen readers misread formatting. Braille translations contain mistakes. We advocate for AI comprehension tools to be treated the same way — acknowledged as imperfect but recognized as valuable, with appropriate safeguards.

**Q: Are you trying to replace human accessibility support?**
A: No. AI comprehension tools supplement human support — they don't replace it. Many people cannot access a human plain-language writer or interpreter when they need to understand a document at 10pm. AI fills the gap. Both/and, not either/or.

**Q: What specific policy change are you seeking?**
A: We are asking federal agencies (DOJ, HHS, ED) to issue guidance confirming that user-authorized AI comprehension tools can qualify as auxiliary aids under the ADA, Section 504, and Section 1557. This is an interpretation of existing law, not new legislation.

## Spokesperson Prep

### Key Messages (memorize these — every interview should include at least one)

1. **"AI comprehension is a civil right, not a tech feature."** This is the frame. Everything flows from it. We're not asking for something new — we're asking institutions to apply existing law to modern tools.

2. **"People are already being harmed."** Hospitals, universities, and government agencies are blocking comprehension tools people depend on. This isn't theoretical.

3. **"The ADA was designed to evolve."** Auxiliary aids went from TTY devices to screen readers to captioned video. AI comprehension tools are the next step in the same progression.

4. **"Everyone deserves to understand."** Our tagline. Use it to close any interview segment or redirect a wandering conversation.

5. **"We're narrowly focused."** We're not talking about all AI uses. We're talking about one specific use case: using AI to understand information that already belongs to you. This focus is what makes the legal argument strong.

### Bridge Phrases (pivot back to key messages)

- "That's an important point, and it connects to something fundamental—"
- "I appreciate that question, because it gets at what makes this different—"
- "Let me give you a concrete example of what that actually looks like—"
- "What matters most here is the person on the other end of that document—"
- "I want to make sure we don't lose sight of what this is really about—"

### Anticipated Hard Questions & Responses

**Q: "Aren't you just an AI industry front group?"**
A: "I understand the concern, and it's a fair question. Here's the answer: our positions are set by disability community advisors and experts, not by sponsors. Our governance has a firewall. But more importantly — look at what we're actually doing. We're building legal doctrine grounded in the ADA. We're documenting stories of real people being harmed. We're convening disability-rights organizations. If an AI company benefits from people having better access to comprehension tools, that's a side effect, not our mission. Our mission is: everyone deserves to understand."

**Q: "AI makes mistakes. Isn't it dangerous to rely on AI for medical or legal comprehension?"**
A: "Every accessibility tool has limitations. Human interpreters make errors. Screen readers misparse content. Braille translations can contain mistakes. We don't withhold those tools because they're imperfect — we improve them and provide safeguards. AI comprehension tools should be held to the same standard: acknowledged as imperfect, but recognized as valuable. The alternative — leaving someone with no tool at all — is more dangerous, not less."

**Q: "Students could use this to cheat."**
A: "We draw a clear line. Using AI to *understand* course material is an accessibility accommodation. Using AI to *produce* work and submit it as your own is academic dishonesty. These are different activities. A student asking AI to explain a textbook passage is doing the same thing as asking a tutor. Universities already allow tutoring. The question is whether AI-assisted comprehension gets the same recognition — and for students with learning disabilities who qualify for accommodations, the answer under Section 504 should be yes."

**Q: "Why should taxpayers / institutions bear this cost?"**
A: "This isn't a new cost — it's a cost we're already paying in worse ways. When a patient misunderstands their discharge instructions and returns to the ER, that costs thousands. When a veteran can't understand their benefits and misses a deadline, the appeals process costs more than a plain-language tool. AI comprehension tools are often free or near-free for the user. The question isn't whether we can afford to provide access — it's whether we can afford not to."

**Q: "The disability community itself is divided on AI. Why should we listen to you?"**
A: "They should listen to people with disabilities — that's our operating principle. We don't speak for the community. We build infrastructure so the community can be heard. Some disability advocates have concerns about AI reliability, consent, and corporate influence. Those concerns are legitimate and we take them seriously. That's why our advisory council centers people with lived experience and why our governance keeps sponsors at arm's length. Disagreement within the community is healthy. What we're trying to prevent is a policy vacuum where nobody is building the framework at all."

### Interview Do's and Don'ts

**Do:**
- Lead with a human story or scenario, then connect to the legal framework
- Name specific institutions or sectors (healthcare, education), not abstract "society"
- Acknowledge criticisms directly before responding — never be defensive
- Use plain language — if you use a legal term, immediately define it
- Say "I don't know" if you don't know — promise to follow up

**Don't:**
- Name specific AI products or companies unless directly asked
- Make claims about AI reliability that can't be backed up
- Speak for the disability community ("people with disabilities believe...") — speak about their rights and your evidence
- Get drawn into debates about AI in general — redirect to comprehension specifically
- Promise specific policy outcomes or timelines
