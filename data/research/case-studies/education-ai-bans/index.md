# Education Case Study: AI Bans, IEPs, and the Accommodation Gap

**Owner:** Research Director | **Status:** Scoping draft v0.5 (anchors identified) | **Started:** 2026-04-07

This is AI-AA's second-strongest sector case study. Education is where the tension between AI policy and disability accommodation is most visible: schools implement blanket AI bans while students with IEPs and 504 plans increasingly depend on AI tools for comprehension. AI detection tools compound the problem by disproportionately flagging non-standard writing patterns — the very patterns produced by dyslexia, learning disabilities, and ESL status.

---

## Executive Summary (Draft)

Students with disabilities are caught in a vicious cycle. They are more likely to use AI tools (72% of students with IEPs/504 plans use generative AI) because they need them for comprehension. They are more likely to be punished for that use by blanket AI bans that don't exempt disability accommodations. And they are more likely to be falsely flagged by AI detection tools that treat non-standard writing as evidence of cheating. The result: the students who most need AI comprehension support are the ones most harmed by policies designed to prevent AI misuse.

## I. The Problem: What We Already Know

### IEP/504 Documents Are Incomprehensible to the Families They're Written For

- Special education documents had readability levels ranging from **9.9 to 12.0 grade levels**
- Parents were reading approximately **3 grade levels below** what the documents required
- Only **13.3% of parents** answered all five IEP comprehension questions correctly; **60% could only answer 1-3 correctly**
- State IEP templates were found to be harder to read than parent handbooks and websites

*Sources:* East Tennessee State University thesis, 2003; JAASEP.

### Students with Disabilities Use AI Because They Need It

- **72%** of students with IEPs/504 plans report using generative AI (CDT, 2024-2025)
- **57%** of special education teachers used AI to help with IEPs/504 plans in 2024-25, up from 39% in 2023-24 (EdWeek, 2025)
- Students with disabilities use AI for comprehension, not cheating — reading support, assignment understanding, navigating school communications

### AI Detectors Disproportionately Harm Students with Disabilities

- **1 in 5 students** said they or someone they know was accused of using AI to cheat without proof (CDT)
- AI detectors are "very likely to falsely flag the writing of non-native English speakers" (Stanford)
- Students with disabilities are **more likely to have been disciplined** for AI use (CDT)
- AI detectors flag non-standard writing patterns — the same patterns produced by dyslexia, learning disabilities, autism, and ESL status
- **79% of parents of students with disabilities** expressed concern about privacy and data security in school AI enforcement

*Sources:* CDT "Up in the Air" 2024-2025; Stanford AI detector bias study; K-12 Dive.

## II. Legal Framework (To Be Developed)

### Key Laws

| Law | Requirement | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **IDEA** | Schools must consider assistive technology in every IEP (20 USC § 1414(d)(3)(B)(v)) | AI comprehension tools may qualify as assistive technology |
| **Section 504** | Schools receiving federal funds must provide reasonable accommodations | AI tool access could be a reasonable accommodation |
| **ADA Title II** | Public schools must not discriminate against students with disabilities | Blanket AI bans that disproportionately affect disabled students may be discriminatory |

### IDEA Assistive Technology Definition — AI Tools Fit

**IDEA Definition (20 U.S.C. § 1401(1)):** "Any item, piece of equipment, or product system, whether acquired commercially off the shelf, modified, or customized, that is used to increase, maintain, or improve functional capabilities of a child with a disability."

This definition is explicitly **technology-neutral** and intentionally broad. Almost any tool qualifies except surgically implanted devices.

**Mandatory AT Consideration (34 CFR § 300.324(a)(2)(v)):** IEP teams must consider "whether the child requires assistive technology devices and services" at **every** IEP meeting.

**OSEP Guidance (January 2024):** "Myths and Facts Surrounding Assistive Technology" — clarifies AT must be considered at every meeting, IEP teams must provide and fully fund AT if needed for FAPE, and the definition gives teams "flexibility to make decisions about appropriate assistive technology devices for individual students." [IDEA AT Guidance](https://sites.ed.gov/idea/idea-files/at-guidance/)

**Legal analysis (NJPSA/FEA 2025):** "When assessing the use of AI for special education students, applicable laws essentially require that its use be considered, as both IDEA and state regulations implementing IDEA mandate that assistive technology be considered and utilized for students with disabilities when necessary for them to receive a free, appropriate public education." The definitions "are broad enough to encompass any technology, from a basic calculator to revolutionary technology such as AI." [NJPSA](https://njpsa.org/the-use-artificial-intelligence-in-meeting-legal-requirements-for-educating-students-with-disabilities/)

**Key legal argument:** AI comprehension tools (summarizing, defining, reformatting text) fall squarely within IDEA's definition of assistive technology — they are "product systems" used to "increase, maintain, or improve functional capabilities" of students with reading-related disabilities. A blanket AI ban that prevents consideration of these tools at IEP meetings may violate 34 CFR § 300.324(a)(2)(v).

### Key Legal Opportunities (No Existing Precedent)

1. **No OCR complaint** has been filed arguing an AI ban violated an IEP/504 plan — AI-AA could file or support the first
2. **No OSEP guidance letter** specifically addresses AI as assistive technology — AI-AA could petition OSEP for clarification
3. **No due process hearing decision** on AI comprehension tools as AT — AI-AA could support a parent's due process claim
4. **Jane Doe v. Michigan** is the first known lawsuit combining AI detection, disability, and ADA — outcome will set precedent

## III. Anchor Cases — IDENTIFIED

### Anchor 1: Jane Doe v. University of Michigan (Filed February 2026) — PRIMARY

**This is a live federal lawsuit explicitly alleging ADA disability discrimination from AI-related academic integrity enforcement.**

A student with documented generalized anxiety disorder (GAD) and obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) — with approved disability accommodations for Great Books 191 (as of September 2025) — was accused **three times** by instructor Theo Nash of using AI to write course papers. The accusations were based on "subjective judgments about Plaintiff's writing style" and "self-confirming 'AI comparison' outputs."

The student provided disability documentation explaining that traits associated with GAD and OCD — "formal tone, meticulous structure, stylistic consistency, and heightened distress during oral confrontation" — "can be misinterpreted as artificial or dishonest behavior and are not proof of AI misuse."

**Legal claims:** ADA violations for failure to "reasonably accommodate" the student's disability-related limitations "in the academic integrity process." NR grade and sanctions blocking graduation.

**Structural parallel to AADJ v. Epic:** Just as Epic's portal architecture creates barriers for disabled patients in healthcare, AI detection and blanket AI policies create barriers for disabled students in education.

**Sources:** [CBS Detroit](https://www.cbsnews.com/detroit/news/university-michigan-student-lawsuit-ai-disability-discrimination/); [EdScoop](https://edscoop.com/u-michigan-student-accused-of-ai-cheating-sues-for-discrimination/); [GovTech](https://www.govtech.com/education/higher-ed/student-sues-university-of-michigan-over-ai-misconduct-accusation)

### Anchor 2: Moira Olmsted / Central Methodist University (2023) — NAMED INDIVIDUAL

Autistic student falsely accused of AI cheating because her "formulaic" neurodivergent writing style triggered AI detectors. Received zero and disciplinary warning. Now screen-records every assignment. See User Story 18 for full details.

**Source:** [Bloomberg, October 2024](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2024-10-18/do-ai-detectors-work-students-face-false-cheating-accusations)

### Anchor 3: NYC Department of Education (January-May 2023) — SYSTEMIC POLICY FAILURE

The nation's largest school district (1.1 million students) imposed a blanket AI ban with **no disability accommodation exemption**, affecting students across ~1,800 schools. Reversed within five months. Chancellor David Banks acknowledged the policy "overlooked the potential."

Also: **Los Angeles Unified**, **Seattle Public Schools**, **Baltimore City Public Schools** all banned ChatGPT in late 2022/early 2023 without documented disability carve-outs. A CRPE scan found only 5% of districts had adopted AI-specific policies by fall 2023.

**Sources:** [Chalkbeat](https://www.chalkbeat.org/newyork/2023/1/3/23537987/nyc-schools-ban-chatgpt-writing-artificial-intelligence/); [NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/chatgpt-ban-dropped-new-york-city-public-schools-rcna85089)

### Supporting: COPAA Cellphone Ban Parallel (September 2024)

COPAA (Council of Parent Attorneys and Advocates) warned that school cellphone bans risk violating IDEA and Section 504 when they limit access to assistive technology. **This is a direct structural parallel to AI bans.** [COPAA](https://www.copaa.org/news/681778/When-School-Policy-Limits-Access-to-Assistive-Technology-for-Students-with-Disabilities.htm)

### Supporting: OCR Guidance on Discriminatory AI Use (November 2024)

U.S. DOE OCR released "Avoiding the Discriminatory Use of Artificial Intelligence" with 21 examples of how AI in schools can produce discriminatory outcomes, including AI-generated 504 plans violating individualized assessment and AI proctoring failing to accommodate disabilities. Establishes OCR will investigate AI-related disability discrimination. [ED.gov](https://www.ed.gov/media/document/avoiding-discriminatory-use-of-artificial-intelligence-112274.pdf)

### Positive Anchor: Franklin Township, Indiana (2024)

Makenzie Gilkison, 14, dyslexic. School district rolled out AI chatbots for special education students in September 2024. Named to National Junior Honor Society. See User Story 12.
