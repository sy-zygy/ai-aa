# Harm Documentation

**Owner:** Research Director

Systematic documentation of cases where blocking AI comprehension access caused measurable harm. This is the most powerful category of evidence — it demonstrates that the problem is not hypothetical.

## Harm Categories

### Medical Harm
Patients who misunderstood medical instructions because they couldn't use AI comprehension tools:
- Medication errors from misunderstood dosing instructions
- Missed follow-up care from incomprehensible discharge summaries
- Uninformed consent due to inability to understand consent forms
- Missed insurance coverage or benefits from incomprehensible notices

### Educational Harm
Students who fell behind because AI comprehension support was blocked:
- Failing grades from inability to comprehend assigned readings
- Dropped courses due to inaccessible materials
- Missed financial aid deadlines from incomprehensible applications
- IEP/504 plan violations where AI comprehension wasn't considered as an accommodation

### Legal Harm
People who suffered legal consequences from misunderstood documents:
- Signed unfavorable contracts without understanding terms
- Missed legal deadlines from incomprehensible notices
- Self-represented litigants unable to navigate court filings
- Evictions or penalties from misunderstood lease terms

### Government Services Harm
People who lost benefits or access due to incomprehensible systems:
- Denied benefits from incorrectly completed applications
- Missed enrollment windows from incomprehensible instructions
- Penalties from misunderstood regulatory communications
- Loss of VA, SSA, or Medicaid benefits from paperwork barriers

### Employment Harm
Employees harmed by inability to comprehend workplace documents:
- Enrolled in wrong benefits plans from incomprehensible materials
- Disciplinary action from misunderstood workplace policies
- Missed opportunities from inaccessible training materials

## Documentation Standard

Each harm case should include:
- **What happened** — The specific harm, described concretely
- **Who was affected** — Person's situation (anonymized)
- **What document caused the barrier** — The specific text that was incomprehensible
- **How AI could have helped** — What a comprehension tool would have done
- **Why AI wasn't available** — Was it blocked by the institution, by the platform, or by lack of access?
- **Measurable impact** — Financial cost, health outcome, educational consequence, legal consequence
- **Source** — How this case was documented

## Documented Cases

### Case 1: AADJ v. Epic Systems — Patient Portal Fragmentation Blocks Disability Benefits Access

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **What happened** | Epic's MyChart system fragments patient records across provider-specific portals with separate logins, making it impossible for patients to compile complete medical histories needed for disability benefits claims. Two-factor authentication is "difficult or impossible" for many disabled people. Third-party tools (including AI) are restricted from aggregating records. |
| **Who was affected** | Larry Miller (severe diabetes complications, applied for disability benefits, claim denied twice for insufficient medical evidence — died while appeal pending; father unable to access son's records). John Hodges (multiple heart attacks, spent 2+ years gathering records from multiple providers, experienced homelessness and lost custody of daughter during the process). |
| **What document caused the barrier** | Medical records fragmented across multiple MyChart portals, each requiring separate authentication |
| **How AI could have helped** | AI tools could aggregate and cross-reference records across portals, identify relevant diagnoses, and compile complete medical evidence for benefits applications |
| **Why AI wasn't available** | Epic's MyChart architecture restricts third-party access. Two-factor authentication creates additional barriers for disabled users. |
| **Measurable impact** | One plaintiff died while appeal was pending. Another experienced homelessness and lost custody of his daughter. |
| **Source** | AADJ v. Epic Systems, filed March 9, 2026, US District Court for the Western District of Texas. [Becker's Hospital Review](https://www.beckershospitalreview.com/healthcare-information-technology/ehrs/epic-sued-over-claims-mychart-fragments-patient-medical-records/) |

### Case 2: Medicaid Unwinding — 25 Million Disenrolled, 70% for Procedural Reasons

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **What happened** | During the Medicaid continuous enrollment unwinding (March 2023 - September 2024), over 25 million people lost Medicaid coverage. Approximately 69-71% were disenrolled for procedural reasons — incomplete paperwork, outdated contact information — not because they were found ineligible. |
| **Who was affected** | Medicaid enrollees nationwide, disproportionately people with disabilities and limited health literacy. The average Medicaid enrollee reads at the 5th-grade level; renewal applications are written at the 11th-18th grade level. |
| **What document caused the barrier** | Medicaid renewal applications and correspondence |
| **How AI could have helped** | AI comprehension tools could have simplified renewal forms, explained requirements in plain language, and helped enrollees complete paperwork correctly |
| **Why AI wasn't available** | No systematic deployment of AI comprehension tools for benefits navigation. Government websites may block AI tools. |
| **Measurable impact** | 25 million people lost health coverage. Procedural denials exceeded 50% in 46 states; exceeded 75% in 19 states. |
| **Source** | KFF Medicaid Unwinding Tracker, 2024. [KFF](https://www.kff.org/medicaid/medicaid-enrollment-and-unwinding-tracker/); GAO-25-107413, 2025. [GAO](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-25-107413) |
