# Research & Evidence

**Owner:** Research Director | **Status:** Active | **Updated:** 2026-04-05

The Research & Evidence department builds the empirical foundation that makes AI-AA's legal arguments credible, its sponsor pitches compelling, and its public narrative human-centered. Without evidence, doctrine is theory. Without stories, policy is abstract.

## Department Scope

This department leads the **Evidence & Research workstream** — one of AI-AA's four charter workstreams.

### What's In Scope

- Documenting user stories of people who rely on AI comprehension tools
- Building sector case studies (healthcare, education, government, legal, employment)
- Documenting harms caused by blocking AI comprehension access
- Researching AI reliability and safety in comprehension contexts
- Recruiting and coordinating the Expert Advisory Group
- Monitoring and documenting opposition positions (ASAN, privacy advocates) to inform counter-arguments
- Maintaining statistics and data on AI accessibility usage

### What's Out of Scope

- Legal analysis (that's Policy's job — we provide the evidence they cite)
- Writing public-facing content (that's Comms' job — we provide the stories they tell)
- Sponsor relationship management (that's Development's job — we provide the proof points they pitch)

## Mission Contribution

Research is the connective tissue between all departments:

| Department | What They Need From Us |
|------------|----------------------|
| **Policy** | Evidence citations for the doctrine memo and guidance petitions. Sector-specific data on where AI comprehension tools are needed and blocked. |
| **Communications** | Human stories for social media content and public narrative. Statistics that make the case visceral and shareable. |
| **Development** | Proof points and impact data for sponsor pitches. Stories that show sponsors their products are already being used as comprehension aids. |
| **Operations** | Advisory council candidates with relevant expertise and credibility. |

## Evidence Categories

### 1. User Stories

Real experiences of people who rely on AI to comprehend written information. Each documented story captures:

- **Who:** The person's situation (disability type, context)
- **What:** The specific comprehension task
- **How AI helped:** What the AI tool did
- **What happened when blocked:** The impact of losing access
- **Quote:** A direct quote capturing the human experience

**Target populations:**
- People with cognitive disabilities navigating medical/legal/financial documents
- Neurodivergent individuals (ADHD, autism, dyslexia) using AI for reading support
- People with acquired brain injuries using AI for comprehension
- Elderly users navigating complex digital systems
- Non-native speakers with learning disabilities

**Target:** 15+ documented stories by Q3 2026

### 2. Sector Case Studies

Institutional contexts where AI comprehension support is needed, blocked, or working:

| Sector | Research Focus | Priority |
|--------|---------------|----------|
| Healthcare | Patient portals blocking AI; AI helping patients understand discharge instructions, consent forms, medication guides | High |
| Education | School AI policies; students with IEPs/504 plans needing AI comprehension support | High |
| Government | Benefits systems (SSA, Medicaid, VA) where AI could help navigate applications | High |
| Legal | Courts and legal-aid settings; self-represented litigants using AI for document comprehension | Medium |
| Employment | Workplace AI policies; employees needing AI for benefits enrollment and policy documents | Medium |

**Target:** 5 sector case studies by Q3 2026

### 3. Harm Documentation

Systematic documentation of cases where blocking AI access caused measurable harm:
- Missed benefits enrollment due to incomprehensible paperwork
- Medical errors from misunderstood instructions
- Educational setbacks from inaccessible materials
- Legal consequences from misunderstood contracts or notices
- Employment problems from inaccessible workplace communications

### 4. Reliability & Safety Research

Addressing the strongest objection — that AI isn't reliable enough:
- Accuracy rates of AI comprehension tools on specific document types
- Comparison to existing auxiliary aids (human interpreters have error rates too)
- Failure modes and proposed safeguards
- Existing peer-reviewed research on AI accuracy in plain-language translation

### 5. Counterpoint Awareness

Understanding and documenting the opposition to inform our arguments:

| Position | Source | Our Response |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| AI is unreliable for plain language | ASAN | We support reliability safeguards. User choice, not mandated replacement. |
| Privacy risks with AI processing sensitive docs | Privacy advocates | We advocate for on-device processing, user authorization, no data retention. |
| AI will replace human accessibility workers | Labor concerns | We position AI as supplement, not replacement. Both/and, not either/or. |

## Operating Model

### How This Department Works

- **Primary sources first.** Published research, official reports, legal filings, agency data. Blog posts and news are leads, not evidence.
- **Human-centered framing.** A single well-documented user story is worth more than abstract statistics for advocacy purposes. Lead with people, support with data.
- **Cite everything.** Every claim must have a traceable source with URL.
- **Update, don't just accumulate.** Research gets stale. Revisit and update quarterly.
- **Feed other teams proactively.** Don't wait for requests. Push relevant evidence to Policy, Comms, and Development as it's gathered.

### Research Quality Standards

- All user stories verified through at least one source (self-report, advocate confirmation, or published account)
- Case studies include specific institutions, policies, and outcomes — not generic sector overviews
- Statistics cite primary research, not secondary summaries
- Counterpoint documentation presents opposing views fairly before responding

### Cross-Department Coordination

- **With Policy:** Align on sector priorities. Provide evidence citations for doctrine memo drafts. Research specific legal questions as needed.
- **With Communications:** Push user stories for content development. Provide statistics for social media. Review public-facing claims for accuracy.
- **With Development:** Provide proof points for sponsor-specific pitches. Document cases where sponsors' products are used as comprehension aids.
- **With Operations:** Identify and recommend advisory council candidates. Vet candidates for conflicts of interest.
